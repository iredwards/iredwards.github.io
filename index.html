I’m learning Category Theory on a lark. It got my attention after reading that it emphasizes looking at the relationships between objects over looking at objects themselves. That is the most valuable insight I absorbed from doing a Math degree long ago.

I’m working my way through Eugenia Cheng’s new book The Joy of Abstraction. I’m also enjoying re-reading it along with the Joy of Abstraction book club that started in February. These are some notes on my journey.

I’ve long been fascinated by Tables of Contents and Indexes. They are useful study aids. Here are some additions I scribbled into the index of the Joyful book. Resources

Category Theory for Programmers by Bartosz Milewski. This excellent lecture series and book approach CT from the perspective of functional programming. You don’t need to be a programmer to follow, but if you’re not carefull you might pick up a bit of Haskell along the way.

Categories for the Working Mathematician by Saunders Mac Lane. Just kidding! I’m not in the target audience of this OG textbook by one of the discovers of CT. But every other book refers to it and it pervades spacetime like the ether. Part of his ToC is here.

Categories for the Idle Mathematician by Richard Borcherds presupposes a background in math, but I caught some glimpses of the road ahead from his lessons.
