Is Category Theory useful or even relevant to the practice of making meaningful content in a corpus findable? I don’t know, but it’s fun learning and asking questions. I’m a Technical Editor by trade, studying Category Theory while my day job is in a funding pause. I’m fascinated by Tables of Contents and Indexes. (See also, Everything is Miscellaneous and its dual Index, A History of the.)

I’m working my way through Eugenia Cheng’s wonderful new book The Joy of Abstraction, which addresses abstraction while introducing Category Theory to the determined reader. By good fortune, the Topos Institute and author Eugenia Cheng started the Joy of Abstraction book club just a few weeks ago, answering questions as we read a chapter each week. (tip: join in!)

These notes grew out of my scribbles in the index of The Joy of Abstraction. It has been a challenge understanding how the parts of CT fit together, and I jump around in the book a lot when a new concept turns out to be related to concept in a previous chapter of which my memory is already fuzzy. I also find it helpful to study different sources, and joke that the third book I read about anything is the best. So I started accumulating notes and links to other sources. As I started to think categorically about these notes, I realized I had some new ways to think about what indexes and ToCs are.

What is Category Theory?

Let’s first look at what a category in CT is not. It’s not a classification, as in a “category 4 hurricane” or books within a certain Dewey Decimal range. Especially when thinking about ToCs, a category doesn’t mean a bunch of related topics. Unless it does.

A category is a collection of objects and relationships between them. There are a few simple rules: associativity (xy = yx) and identity (1x = x). The Wikipedia article on CT lays it out in a few paragraphs. From these simple foundations, CT provides a framework for reasoning about abstractions while remaining clear on what has been abstracted away. CT is so simple it’s hard to write about, yet so rigorous and adaptable that it serves as the “mathematics of mathematics”. Don’t settle for my naive math tourist’s explanation when there are so many accounts by real experts. I’ll focus on making those resources findable.

Resources I like

Tables of Contents for these are collected here.

The Joy of Abstraction by Eugenia Cheng is usually the first and last I read on a given subject.

Category Theory for Programmers by Bartosz Milewski. This excellent lecture series and book approach CT from the perspective of functional programming. You don’t need to be a programmer to follow, but if you’re not careful you might pick up a bit of Haskell along the way.

Categories for the Working Mathematician by Saunders Mac Lane. Just kidding! I’m not in the target audience of this OG textbook by one of the discovers of CT. But every other book refers to it and it pervades spacetime like the ether. Part of his ToC is here.

Categories for the Idle Mathematician by Richard Borcherds presupposes a background in math, but I caught some glimpses of the road ahead from his lessons.

Category Theory for Beginners by Richard Southwell
